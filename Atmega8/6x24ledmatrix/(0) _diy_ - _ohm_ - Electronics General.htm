>I'm new to electronics, where do I get started?

There are several good books that are commonly recommended for beginners and those wanting to learn more. There are also plenty of good Youtube channels that teach about the basics as well as advanced concepts. The best way to get involved in electronics is just to make stuff. Don't be afraid to get your hands dirty. Take something apart or build something you find cool on one of the many electronics websites.

>What books are there?

Beginner:

Getting Started in Electronics by Forrest Mims III
Make: Electronics by Charles Platt
How to Diagnose and Fix Everything Electronic by Michael Jay Greier

Intermediate:

All New Electronics Self-Teaching Guide by Harry Kybett, Earl Boysen
Practical Electronics for Inventors by Paul Scherz and Simon Monk

Advanced:

The Art of Electronics by Paul Horowitz

No, 172.8Ah at 3.7V would be nearly 640Wh. At an average draw of 7.2W, you only need 7.2Wx24h=172.8Wh. I'd call it 200Wh to be on the safe side. That's a 54Ah for a 3.7V cell. You'll need a DC/DC converter, either way, though, since a single bank of 3.7V is too little and two banks in series for 7.4V is too much.

There's no point in using a linear regulator to drop 7.4V down to 5V. That's just wasting a bunch of your capacity.

It's obvious from your post however, that you don't know even the basics about this. It's not trivially easy to build a decent battery pack like this without already knowing what you're doing, at least out of lithium. They're temperamental, and need balancing circuitry to keep them from destroying each other over time or, if you use more volatile chemistries, destroying each other quite violently. I'd suggest starting by reading around regarding exactly what the various ratings mean, what different types are available, and what kind of charging schema they need before thinking about how to make a pack out of them.

It's probably fucked beyond repair.
When left uncharged for large amounts of times, crystals start growing on the plates and impede any kind of current to flow (because the internal resistance went up drastically).
You can try charging it at 13.8 or 14.4 volts but you probably won't have any current flowing (you can check this with a multimeter).

Now, there is a way to get them working again.
You have to force high voltages (>100v) into the terminals.
But you have to do this in very short pulses !
This helps redissolving the crystals and can (in certain cases) help revive the battery to an *almost* working state.

Why short pulses ? Because high voltages will cause bubbles of gas to form by electrolysis and you'll fuck up your battery or blow it up. Short pulses ensure you get the current flowing but shortly enough that bubbling is not a problem.

You can check for "battery desulfator" online but I'd advise you throw that battery away.
I've had about a dozen sealed ups batteries in the same condition and could only get one to a working state.

>Whats the safetiest way to deal with caps?
Use low voltages. If you're dealing with low voltages and itty bitty jelly bean capacitors you'll never hurt yourself.

But if you're dealing with high voltages, put resistors across the terminals of caps so that when power is shut off they bleed off their energy reliably and safely.

Don't put your booger hook across the leads of high voltage caps.

Don't charge them at higher voltages than they are spec'd for.

Don't charge polarized (electrolytic) caps with incorrect polarity.

>Are there substitute for capacitors (capacitor-less) circuits?
No.
